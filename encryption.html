Encryption

Encryption ensures the confidentiality, integrity, and the non-repudiation of data encrypted. The term encryption comes from the Greek word kryptos. Encryption is an old concept, the use of encryption dates back to 1900 BC, when the Egyptians used encryptions in their scribes to conceal a message. It was also used by the Spartans and Romans, in the middle ages and battlefields.
Now, encryption protects our most valuable information such as bank information and our personal details, it is also used to prevent the reproduction of copyrighted material. Encryption hides our deepest secrets, regardless of how dark they are. Encryption is not limited to providing confidentiality, it is also embedded into key fobs such as that of a car, the most common algorithm for key fobs is rolling code.
Encryption warrants the integrity of the data encrypted to for it be non-repudiable. The degree of integrity depends on the encryption procedure and the measures implemented to reinforce encryption. When integrity is a concern, encryption is usually paired with a digital signature which certifies the authenticity and validity of the data

How it works

Encryption works by translating plain text into random characters which can only be reverted by entering the encryption key. The way data is encrypted differs as there are a variety of algorithms available, those algorithms can be categorised into symmetric or asymmetric encryption. The reliability of the security of encrypted data is reliant on the length of the key and how the algorithm handles vulnerabilities.

Symmetric encryption

Symmetric encryption is much faster than asymmetric encryption since it uses the same key for encrypting and decrypting. AES is a common symmetric algorithm which was adopted to replace DES as it was an easy target to brute force attacks. The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) formulated this algorithm so that it will be easy to insert into hardware and software. AES h as proven its reliability as it is used by governments to conceal classified information. The sender must send the key so that the recipient can decipher it. To securely send the key, it is encrypted using asymmetric encryption.

Asymmetric encryption

Instead of using one key, asymmetric cryptography, or also known as public-key cryptography involves the use of two different linked keys, one public and one private. RSA is a favoured algorithm as both public and private keys can encrypt, and the remaining key is used to decrypt.

Issues and vulnerabilities

As all forms of security unfortunately have their vulnerabilities or loopholes. A method attackers can breach encryption is through brute force, which involves trying every possible key combination. Attackers can also locate flaws in the system design, which is referred to as a side-channel attack. This is similar to cryptanalysis, which is the method of finding a weakness in the cipher.
