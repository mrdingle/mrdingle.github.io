Biometric Devices

Biometric devices take advantage of the characteristics of the human body and behaviour. Since no two humans are completely the same, the basis of biometric devices is of how a person either interacts with the device, or how the device interacts with the person. The use of biometric devices opens up countless possibilities in the world of network security as not only will it be able to verify the identity of a human, it is a more convenient alternative to usernames and passwords and physical objects like key fobs. Physical verification

Fingerprint and handprint verification, facial recognition, and DNA are examples of physical biometric identifiers. Fingerprint and facial recognition is already present in mobile devices, in which the general public can use. There are two ways that fingerprints can be imaged.

Optical Scanner

An optical scanner uses a bright light, either a charge-coupled device (CCD) or a CMOS image sensor over the fingerprint, it works similar to a digital photograph. The image is then translated to code using pattern-matching software and it is stored into a database.

Capacitative Scanner

A capacitive scanner determines the pattern of a fingerprint by imaging the protruding ridges of a fingerprint when it is pressed on the scanner. As with the optical scanner, the image is then converted to code and stored. Fingerprints cannot be duplicated as they are stored in an algorithm, and that algorithm cannot be reverted into an image.

Verification through behaviour

Psychological, or behavioural identifiers focus more on human capabilities. Gestures and voice recognition are a common solution to unlocking devices. Two factor authentications in the form of email verification prevents spam or abuse as it will require the user to have a genuine email and to take time to verify it. Captcha, or similar variations of it deters bots as it requires comprehension or the imperfections of humans, in which both cases, some bots may not be able to achieve.
