Network Security

Data is not only a source of information, it can be used as a weapon against people and organisations. Data such as medical records, confidential political matters, personal information, bank accounts can inflict certain degrees of damage.
Therefore, the security of a network is paramount to prevent misuse or unauthorised changes to the network and the data within it. Network security may be an uninteresting topic, but it is the most complex and critical aspect of network administration.

The principles of network security

The concept of network security encompasses the three principles of: confidentiality, integrity and availability, collectively known as the CIA triad or the AIC triad to avoid confusion with the Central Intelligence Agency. The triad is a model that guides organisations into creating and maintaining a truly secure network.

Confidentiality

Confidentiality is similar to, and integrates with the concept of privacy. It is a series policies and protocols that provide certain individuals access to data within a network based on a criterion.
The criteria are enforced by various measures that serve as a filter to ensure that data is not disclosed to unauthorised persons, while authorised individuals can still view and access the data. The data can also be categorised by the amount of damage it will generate and the security measures adjusted accordingly.

Security measures are not only limited to software. Since computers are incapable to attacks that involve human interaction, namely social engineering. Training authorised individuals will expose them to the various ways that attackers can manipulate them into disclosing data or the access to that data, should they be a victim of an attack, they would be able to effectively react and comprehend the safest and quickest way to prevent damage.
In addition to countering social engineering attacks, users can prevent it by having a strong password or storing data in air gapped computers, external storage devices or in hard copy.

Integrity

Integrity correlates with the validity, reliability, consistency and credibility of data. There are two objectives to ensure that the data is kept accurate.

  1. Unauthorised users must not be able to modify the data
  2. The external and internal consistency of data is maintained

To enforce these protocols and prevent the unauthorised modification and inconsistency of data networks have backups or redundancies set in place to restore data if an attack had occurred or an event such as an electromagnetic pulse or a server crash.

Availability

The availability of a network relates to how regularly and thoroughly hardware is maintained. This revolves around the actions of:

  • Prioritising the repair of any conflicts or hardware fault if they arise
  • Necessary system upgrades
  • The implementation or augmentation of security systems to prevent attacks
  • Backups of data in a secure location
  • The capacity of the network to be able to efficiently communicate without fault

Schools, organisations and governments rely on security systems and training to prevent unauthorised access to a network or data, as well as protecting the users of the networks from attacks such as denial of service or DDoS, SSL, viruses, malware, worms, and social engineering attacks like baiting, phishing, pretexting, scareware, and ransomware.
